Homework 4 & 7: Analogies and Mathematical Properties

Analogies: LLN vs. Random Walk

Homework 7 (the simulation of a Random Walk) shares a deep mathematical foundation with Homework 4 (the Law of Large Numbers, or LLN). Both are, at their core, simulations of a Bernoulli process.

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of $n$ independent trials, where each trial has exactly two outcomes (e.g., success/failure, secure/breached), and the probability of success remains constant for every trial (at least, in Model 1 of HW 7).

Similarities

  • Bernoulli Foundation: Both models are built on $n$ independent trials.
    • HW 4 (LLN): A "trial" is a coin flip, with outcomes {Success, Failure} and probability $p$.
    • HW 7 (Random Walk): A "trial" is a week, with outcomes {Secure, Breached} and probability
      $q = (1-p)^m$.
  • Binomial Core: In both models (assuming Model 1 of HW 7), the total number of "successes" ($k$ heads in HW 4, $k$ secure weeks in HW 7) follows a Binomial Distribution, $k \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$. Model 2 of HW 7 (decreasing attackers) is a generalization known as a Poisson Binomial Distribution, as the probability $q_w$ is no longer identical for each trial.
  • Central Limit Theorem: As $n$ (the number of trials/weeks) diverges, the resulting distributions in both simulations (Binomial and Poisson Binomial) converge to a Normal (Gaussian) Distribution. This is why the histograms take on a "bell curve" shape for large $n$.

Revised Analysis: Key Differences

Key Differences: Average vs. Displacement

The primary difference between the two assignments is not in the underlying process, but in what is being measured and what concept is being demonstrated.

Feature HW 4: Law of Large Numbers (LLN) HW 7: Random Walk (Server Security)
Metric Measured The Average (relative frequency): $\bar{X}_n = k/n$ The Cumulative Score (displacement): $S_n = 2k - n$
Core Question "What is the true probability of the process?" "How far from the origin has the process drifted?"
Concept Demonstrated Convergence & Estimation: The average $\bar{X}_n$ converges to a single, stable value (the true probability $p$). It demonstrates stability. Diffusion & Volatility: The score $S_n$ tends to drift further and further away from zero. It demonstrates cumulative deviation.
Trajectory Plot Trajectories converge "in a funnel" toward a single horizontal line (the probability $p$). Trajectories "fan out" and spread, moving away from the origin.
Final Histogram Shows the distribution of final averages, $f(n)$. As $n$ increases, this histogram becomes narrower. Shows the distribution of final scores, $S_n$. As $n$ increases, this histogram becomes wider.

Mathematical Properties and Relationships

The distributions generated in these exercises are intrinsically linked to fundamental concepts in combinatorics.

Binomial Coefficients

A combinatorial coefficient, specifically the binomial coefficient, answers the question: "How many different paths (trajectories) result in the same final score?"

The number of ways to get exactly $k$ "secure" weeks in $n$ total weeks is given by "n choose k":

$$\binom{n}{k} = C(n, k) = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$

This is precisely why the Binomial Distribution (from Model 1) has its name. The probability of any single path with $k$ successes is $q^k (1-q)^{n-k}$. Since there are $\binom{n}{k}$ such paths, the total probability of having $k$ successes is:

$$P(k) = \binom{n}{k} q^k (1-q)^{n-k}$$

Pascal's Triangle

Pascal's Triangle is a recursive visualization of the binomial coefficients. Each number in the triangle is the sum of the two numbers directly above it: $C(n, k) = C(n-1, k-1) + C(n-1, k)$.

This triangle is also a direct map of a symmetric random walk ($q=0.5$). The value at row $n$, position $k$, literally counts the number of distinct paths a walker can take from the origin (0,0) to reach that point in $n$ steps. The "score" $S = 2k - n$ is just a re-labeling of the $k$-th position in row $n$.

The numbers in Pascal's Triangle (e.g., at row 4: 1, 4, 6, 4, 1) show how many paths lead to each point after 4 steps.

Binomial Expansion

The Binomial Expansion is the algebraic generator for these probabilities and coefficients. It defines how to expand a power of a binomial:

$$(x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k$$

The connection becomes clear if we set $x = (1-q)$ (probability of -1) and $y = q$ (probability of +1):

$$((1-q) + q)^n = 1^n = 1$$

This expansion demonstrates that the sum of the probabilities of all possible outcomes is 1. Each term in the sum, $\binom{n}{k} (1-q)^{n-k} q^k$, is precisely the probability of ending up in the state with $k$ successes (which corresponds to a score of $S = 2k - n$).

The Fibonacci Sequence

A more subtle connection exists with the Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ...). While not directly counting the paths in this specific random walk, it appears in related combinatorial problems (e.g., counting paths on a line that can take steps of length 1 or 2).

The most famous link is visual: the sums of the "shallow diagonals" of Pascal's Triangle are the Fibonacci numbers. This elegantly ties all these concepts together: the Random Walk is mapped by Pascal's Triangle, whose coefficients are defined by the Binomial Expansion, and whose diagonals sum to the Fibonacci sequence.

Summing the diagonals of Pascal's Triangle reveals the Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ...).
