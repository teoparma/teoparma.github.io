Measures of Location

Averages, also called measures of central tendency or measures of location, summarize a dataset with a single value that describes the center or typical score. They can be computed in various methods: the most common, the Arithmetic Mean, is calculated by adding all the numbers together and the dividing by the count of numbers; the Median (the middle value in a sorted list) and the Mode (the most frequently occurring value) as well as Weighted averages, Geometric and Harmonic.

Depending on the type of data and its distribution (e.g., the presence of outliers), different averages highlight different aspects of the phenomenon. This is why some prove to be more useful and representative than others for describing that dataset.

Measures of Dispersion

Measures of Dispersion, also called measures of variability or measures of spread, summarize a dataset with a single value that describes how spread out or scattered the data points are. They are a vital complement to measures of central tendency, as they indicate the "risk" or "uncertainty" in the data.

They can be computed in various methods: the simplest, the Range, is calculated by subtracting the minimum value from the maximum value. More robust and common measures include the Quartile Deviation (the spread of the middle 50% of data), the Variance (the average of squared deviations from the mean), the Standard Deviation (the square root of the variance), and the Mean Deviation (the average of absolute deviations from the mean).

The choice of which measure of dispersion to use depends entirely on the type of data you have (scale of measurement) and the shape of its distribution (especially its sensitivity to outliers).

Statistical Distributions

Let's see how the different ways to calculate location and dispersion relate with different distributions. First a quick explanation of various kinds of distributions.

Exponential Distribution

The exponential distribution is a right-skewed distribution used primarily to model the waiting time until a specific event occurs in a process where events happen continuously and independently at a constant average rate.

It is defined only for positive real numbers and its probability density function starts high at x=0 and then decays exponentially as x increases.

Key Property: Memoryless: This is its most distinctive feature. It means that the probability of an event happening in the future is independent of how much time has already passed.

Applications:

  • Time between customer arrivals at a service desk.
  • Time until a component or machine failure (reliability engineering).
  • Time between radioactive decays.

Cauchy Distribution

Also known as the Lorentz distribution, the Cauchy distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped distribution, but it's infamous in statistics for its extreme properties. It is characterized by having heavy tails, meaning extreme values (outliers) occur much more frequently than in a Normal distribution.

Key Property: The most significant characteristic is that its mean, variance, and all higher moments are undefined (they mathematically diverge to infinity). This is due to its heavy tails.

Applications:

  • Modeling physical phenomena like the resonance energy in spectroscopy (known as the Breit-Wigner distribution in physics).
  • Modeling data with frequent and significant outliers (robust statistics).
  • It is the distribution of the ratio of two independent standard normal random variables.

Normal Distribution

The Normal distribution, or Gaussian distribution (often called the "bell curve"), is the most widely used and important distribution in statistics due to the Central Limit Theorem: It is perfectly symmetric and bell-shaped, with a range over all real numbers.

Key Property: The CLT states that the distribution of sample means (or sums) from any distribution will approach a Normal distribution as the sample size increases.

Applications:

  • Modeling natural phenomena that result from the sum of many small, independent effects (e.g., human height, measurement errors, test scores).
  • The foundation for much of inferential statistics (confidence intervals, hypothesis testing).
  • Approximating other distributions (like Binomial or Poisson) under certain conditions.

Log-Normal Distribution

The Log-Normal distribution is a continuous distribution for a random variable whose logarithm is normally distributed. It is defined only for positive real numbers and is right-skewed. It is used for quantities that cannot be negative and that tend to grow multiplicatively.

Applications:

  • Finance: Modeling stock prices, as prices cannot fall below zero and tend to grow exponentially.
  • Modeling sizes: such as the size of organisms, income distribution, or particle sizes.
  • Failure times in reliability analysis where the failure rate is not constant.
